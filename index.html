I conduct substantive and methodological research motivated by two questions:

  • What role do ordinary people play in producing and preventing crime and violence?
  • How can we enable and encourage people to undertake costly, prosocial behaviors that prevent crime, mitigate the consequences of conflict, and improve governance?

I use ideas from social psychology and comparative politics, as well as insights from field research, to construct new theories. I have conducted fieldwork in Nigeria, South Africa, and elsewhere.

To test these ideas, I develop and apply new methods and conduct field research and experiments.

My methodological work has led to a new way of learning about and improving research designs, as well as new designs and analysis strategies for survey experiments to study sensitive questions. I have published widely-used, award-winning software to implement these methods.

My research appears in two book manuscripts, one under advanced contract at Princeton University Press and one under review. My papers have been published in leading journals such as the American Political Science Review, American Journal of Political Science, Journal of the American Statistical Association, Political Analysis, and Science Advances. I am grateful for funding from the Hewlett Foundation, the Arnold Foundation, the National Science Foundation, and the U.K. Department for International Development, among others.

I received my Ph.D. in 2016 from Princeton University and was a pre/postdoctoral fellow with Evidence in Governance and Politics at Columbia University before starting at UCLA.

Advising


I formally advise a number of Ph.D. students in political science. I meet weekly in the Politics of Order and Development Lab with all of my students. If you think you might be a good fit, send me an email and set up a time to meet. I'm happy to talk to other UCLA Ph.D. students about research even if I'm not advising you -- you can sign up for office hours. Email me if you can't find a time.

Prospective graduate students: I actively advise and collaborate with graduate students in the political science Ph.D. program at UCLA. Our department, like most in political science, does not admit students to work with specific faculty. Admissions decisions are made by a committee, which I am not currently sitting on. However, you are welcome to mention my name in your personal statement in order to ensure it is sent to me during the admission process. I encourage you to read Jessica Calarco's excellent Field Guide to Grad School and read advice from Chris Blattman and Macartan Humphreys. Following the example of Betsy Paluck, I no longer have personal conversations with prospective students, in order to avoid favoring students who have received advice to connect with faculty or who have connections with my colleagues. If you are admitted, I will be eager to talk about working with you at UCLA.
